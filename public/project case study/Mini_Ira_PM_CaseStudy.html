01 · Problem

Wellness apps are intimidating exactly when you need them most.

When someone is anxious at 1AM, they don't want to fill a PHQ-9 form, schedule a session, or read a 6-step guide on box breathing. They want a human voice. They want something to do with their hands. They want the loop in their head to stop, even for 60 seconds.

Clinical tone barrier

Bullet-pointed advice from a chatbot lands like a pamphlet. It widens the gap rather than closing it.

Decision fatigue

"Pick a meditation / pick a breathing exercise / pick a journal prompt." Distressed users don't want to pick.

No physical escape valve

Talk-only apps trap users in their own head. Sometimes you need something tactile to reset.

Memory amnesia

Most chatbots forget you the second you close the tab. There's no "we" — only repeated strangers.

06 · Features

Deep dive into what we shipped.

Core

Ira — the voice

Every prompt enforces a "best friend texting" persona. 1–4 sentences. Emojis welcome. No bullets, no numbered lists, no clinical phrasing. Ira matches the user's energy — terse if they're terse, expansive if they open up.

User value
Talking to Ira feels like talking to a person — not consulting a tool.
AI

Real-time emotion tagging

Every assistant reply begins with a hidden [EMOTION:xxx] tag. The frontend strips it, displays the corresponding mood chip, and logs it to the user's emotional history. Six states: happy, sad, stressed, excited, lonely, neutral.

Why it matters
Powers personalization, pattern detection, and the right-game-at-the-right-time logic.
Differentiator

Mood-triggered 3D games

Two purpose-built micro-games rendered inline in the chat:

  • 🏃 Endless Runner — for stressed, anxious, happy, excited, neutral. Burn or ride the energy.
  • 🫧 Bubble Pop — for sad, lonely. Soft, satisfying, low-stakes.

Ira chooses based on detected mood. The user never sees a picker.

PM bet
A 30-second physical loop interrupts rumination better than another text reply.
Memory

Future-self messages

In good moments, users write a note to their future self. Ira holds it, and gently surfaces it later when the system detects 2+ recent low-mood interactions. The user becomes their own caregiver.

Magic moment
A line you wrote yourself, returning exactly when you need it.
Insights

Mood dashboard & history

Personal trend view across the last 20 interactions. No scoring, no judgment — just visibility. Ira also adapts its tone when negative-mood streaks build up.

Outcome
Self-awareness without anxiety.
Continuity

Conversations that persist

Authenticated users get conversation history, sidebar navigation, and the ability to resume where they left off. Ira remembers your name once shared.

Why
A friend who forgets you isn't a friend.

07 · Decisions

Key product decisions & the trade-offs behind them.

Ira picks the game, not the user

Trade-off: less control, more magic.

Distressed users hate menus. Letting Ira auto-route to Endless Runner or Bubble Pop based on detected mood removes friction at the worst possible moment.

Cut Zen Ball — kept only 2 games

Trade-off: less variety, more polish.

Three half-good games > two great games — wrong. Two games map cleanly to the two emotional clusters that matter most: high-arousal vs low-arousal distress.

Killed the "1-sentence only" rule

Trade-off: longer replies, warmer experience.

Forcing Ira to one sentence felt robotic. Users want a friend who actually responds — not a fortune cookie.

Lovable Cloud over self-hosting

Trade-off: platform lock-in, faster shipping.

For an MVP testing emotional product-market fit, days-to-first-user beats infrastructure purity.

Hidden emotion tags vs visible scoring

Trade-off: less transparency, more dignity.

Showing users their "anxiety score" creates anxiety. We tag silently, surface gently in the dashboard.

Auth required for memory, not for try

Trade-off: some friction, full data integrity.

Anyone can land and read. Sending a message gates auth — protecting RLS-secured emotional data while keeping the door open.

11 · Learnings

What I'd carry into the next product.

1 · Tone is a feature, not a polish pass.

The single biggest unlock wasn't a new feature — it was deleting the "one sentence only" rule. The product became warmer overnight. In emotional products, tone is the product.

2 · Remove choice when users are dysregulated.

Letting Ira pick the game (instead of showing a menu) was a small change with outsized UX impact. Defaults are kindness.

3 · Micro-interventions {'>'} long content.

A 30-second tactile game beats a 5-minute meditation for users in acute states. Build for the worst minute of someone's day.

4 · Scope discipline kept the soul.

Saying no to a third game, an avatar, voice mode, and journaling templates kept the product readable. An MVP that does one feeling well beats one that does five things mediocrely.
