Product Design · AI · Travel

Agoda — AI Travel Assistant

Designing an end-to-end automated travel planner that uses AI to attract new users, optimise itinerary creation, and make reservations easier — without becoming yet another conventional chatbot.

Problem statement

Build a completely automated travel planner for Agoda that reduces planning time and decision fatigue. The assistant should not be a conventional chatbot; it should improve the trip experience by handling search, comparison, booking and in-trip queries through natural conversation.

Understanding the user

NameRehman Sharma, 34, Marketing Manager (New Delhi)
ProfileHigh tech-savvy; travels frequently for business and leisure
GoalsSearch & book flights and stays · Plan activities · Handle issues fast
ChallengesLimited time · Hard to compare options · Too many platforms
NeedsSmooth UX · Personalised recommendations · Reliable customer support

Jobs To Be Done

When I plan a trip and need to book flights, hotels, activities and transport, but it's difficult to manage these across different platforms, please help me by offering a unified solution to book and organise everything in one place, so I can streamline my planning and enjoy a hassle-free trip.

Solution — AgodaGuru assistant

AgodaGuru is discovered organically on the homepage. Users can either type a free-form prompt or pick from suggestion bubbles. The flow handles errors gracefully — every limitation is reframed as an opportunity (e.g., when historical data is unavailable, the assistant pivots to ranking past destinations instead).

  • Homepage → discovers assistant → user input or suggestion bubble
  • Response → follow-up question → decision point
  • Errors are converted into suggestive next questions
  • Conversation is auto-saved as a thread for continuity

YouTube — UX through Nielsen's 10 Heuristics

Auditing YouTube — the world's second-largest search engine — using Jakob Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics, with concrete examples of where the design wins and where it slips.

YouTube reaches an enormous, diverse user base across devices and languages. Small interface decisions compound into measurable engagement and retention — making it a perfect canvas for a heuristic audit.

Selected heuristics in practice

1 · Visibility of System Status

Double-tap to skip plays a 10-second animation that scales with repeat taps. Toast messages confirm every action — captions on, screen locked, saved to Watch Later.

2 · Match with the Real World

Familiar terms — Trending, Subscriptions, Watch Later — mirror real-world language. Toggles visibly indicate On/Off; no guesswork.

3 · User Control & Freedom

"Not interested" and "Don't recommend channel" let users actively shape their feed. Minimised view lets users explore while a video plays.

4 · Consistency & Standards

Captions, autoplay, like, playback speed and quality stay in the same position across Shorts, the standard player and desktop.

5 · Error Prevention

Removing a video from Watch Later introduces friction so users can re-save. Handle name changes show a 14-day caution dialog.

9 · Recover from Errors

On network errors, a playful astronaut illustration appears with a clear path to Downloads — turning a dead-end into a useful next action.

The gap

Watch Later still hides removed videos as [Private Video] or [Deleted video] with no way to recover the title — a recoverable error left unrecoverable.

What I'd improve

  • Surface contextual help on errors instead of hiding it under Account → Settings → About → Help.
  • Make Watch Later recoverable: store last-known title and channel even after a video is deleted.
  • Add a lightweight recently-removed log so users can trace what they had saved.

Takeaway · Heuristic audits are growth tools

Even mature products like YouTube leave clear UX wins on the table. A structured heuristic audit turns subjective gripes into a prioritised, defensible improvement backlog.

Competitive Teardown · Reviews

Nykaa vs. Purplle — Rating & Review Features

A side-by-side teardown of how India's two leading beauty marketplaces collect, structure and surface customer reviews — and what each could learn from the other.

Nykaa — strengths

  • Only verified buyers can review — credibility.
  • Reward points incentivise honest reviews.
  • Upvotes float useful reviews to the top.
  • Photo upload encouraged with playful CTA.

Purplle — strengths

  • "What customers say" % — quick holistic view.
  • Green/red tags surface positives & negatives.
  • Click-format follow-ups reduce review effort.
  • Detailed image upload guidance.

Cross-pollination opportunities

Nykaa should borrow

Predefined attributes like Value for Money, Effectiveness, Packaging — to standardise feedback. Add a filter by skin/hair type so shoppers find relevant reviews fast.

Purplle should borrow

Restrict reviews to verified purchasers to lift trust. Match Nykaa's filter & sort richness on keywords and attributes.

Universal recommendations

  • Video reviews: short-form video uploads — powerful for textures, swatches, finishes.
  • Brand response loop: brands reply publicly to reviews — converts complaints into trust signals.
  • Review-of-the-week spotlight: reward and showcase the most helpful reviewers monthly.

Why this matters · Reviews are growth infrastructure

For beauty marketplaces, the review feed is the product page. A 1% lift in review submission rate compounds into measurably higher conversion, lower returns and stronger SEO.

How Snapchat's strategically-designed notification system keeps 414M daily users tethered to the app — and where over-notification is starting to backfire.

Personas

Jayden Roberts · 21 · Digital Media student, LA

Goals: Stay connected, experiment with AR lenses, communicate in project groups.
Pain: Notification fatigue during study, multimedia data usage, content overload.

Zara Patel · 27 · Fashion Influencer, Mumbai

Goals: Grow brand via Stories & Spotlight, monetise via partnerships.
Pain: Opaque algorithm, constant notification noise, high category saturation.

Notification taxonomy

From friends

  • Friend is typing
  • New chat message
  • Friend opened your Snap
  • Friend screenshotted your Snap/chat
  • Friend uploaded a story
  • Streak about to expire

From the system & feed

  • Memory snaps & app updates
  • Suggested friend from contacts
  • Birthday / festival wishes
  • Influencer posted a story
  • New lens or filter by influencer
  • Friend request received / accepted

What Snapchat does well

  • Engaging interface: AR lenses, filters and disappearing messages create a creative, low-stakes loop.
  • Privacy-first messaging: ephemeral content reduces digital-footprint anxiety.
  • Snap Maps & Stories: social presence without the LinkedIn-heaviness.
  • Frequent updates keep younger cohorts engaged.

Where it goes wrong

Snapchat — shut up, dear God. I can't go 30 minutes without a notification for everything. 'Try this new filter', 'Remember this?', 'Random person you've never met just posted a story'… Also, stop sending me notifications of the same story multiple times a day.— Verbatim user quote

Recommendations

  • Notification budget: per-user daily cap with smart prioritisation — friend snaps > streak alerts > influencer fluff.
  • De-duplicate story alerts: a friend's story should never trigger more than one push.
  • Customisable My AI: let users opt-out or scope it down without burying the toggle.
  • Camera & video QoS: invest in device-tier optimisation.
  • Personalisation by ML: tune the mix by recent in-app behaviour, not category defaults.

Metrics

North Star

Monthly Active Users — single most important measure of platform vitality.

L1

Notification CTR · Open rate · Conversion rate (post-engagement actions).

L2

Streak retention · Time-to-respond on chat · Avg snaps sent per DAU.

Counter-metric

Push opt-out rate — the single best signal that Snapchat is over-notifying.
